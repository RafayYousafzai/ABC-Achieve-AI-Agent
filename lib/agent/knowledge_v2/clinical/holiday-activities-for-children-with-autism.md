Skip to content Fun and Inclusive Holiday Activities for Children with Autism ADAM August 19, 2025 6 mins read Table of Contents The holiday season is a time for joy, family, and making memories. For families with children on the autism spectrum, however, holidays can bring challenges such as sensory overload, changes in routine, and stressful social situations. With thoughtful planning, holidays can still be fun and inclusive. By focusing on each child’s strengths, preferences, and comfort, families can create meaningful traditions together. This guide highlights practical, autism-friendly holiday activities that parents, caregivers, and educators can use to create a season filled with warmth and connection. Why Inclusive Holidays Matter for Children with Autism Every child experiences holidays differently. Children with autism may have unique needs when it comes to sensory input, communication, and transitions. By tailoring holiday activities, families can: Reduce stress and meltdowns Encourage social interaction and bonding Support sensory regulation Create positive and lasting memories Taking a proactive approach helps ensure children with autism feel comfortable, included, and celebrated during this special time. Sensory-Friendly Decorations Decorating can be magical, but flashing lights and loud sounds may be overwhelming. Instead, choose soft lights, textured ornaments, or calming colors. Involve your child in hanging decorations so they feel part of the tradition. 2. Holiday Crafts Simple, structured crafts are wonderful for children with autism. Try making paper snowflakes, decorating stockings, or creating hand-painted ornaments. These holiday activities for kids with autism promote fine motor skills and provide a sense of accomplishment. 3. Baking Together Baking cookies or simple holiday treats can be a fun, multisensory activity. Let your child measure, stir, or decorate using sprinkles. The smells, textures, and tastes make it a sensory-rich holiday activities experience. 4. Music and Singing Many children enjoy holiday music. Keep the volume comfortable and consider using calming playlists. Singing together or using hand movements with songs adds a fun, interactive element. Story Time Traditions Reading holiday-themed books with visual supports or social stories helps children prepare for new experiences. Choose interactive books with textures or flaps to keep them engaged. 6. Adaptive Holiday Movies Watching movies at home is a cozy way to celebrate. Dim the lights, use captions, and give your child control over breaks if they need them. 7. Outdoor Winter Fun Fresh air and movement are grounding. Activities like building a snowman, walking through a decorated neighborhood, or visiting a sensory-friendly holiday lights display are great low-pressure options. 8. Virtual Celebrations If large gatherings feel overwhelming, connect with loved ones through short video calls. This allows your child to participate socially while staying in their comfort zone. 9. Sensory Bins with a Holiday Twist Create bins filled with rice, beans, cotton balls (as “snow”), or small holiday toys. Sensory play supports regulation and can be both calming and joyful. 10. Quiet Time Spaces Designate a calm holiday-themed space where your child can unwind. Include soft blankets, dim lighting, or fidget toys. This provides a safe retreat if celebrations feel overwhelming. Is sensory overload making holiday celebrations stressful? Visit a Sensory-Friendly Santa Many communities now host autism sensory holiday activities such as quiet Santa visits. These events are less crowded, calmer, and welcoming for children with autism. 12. Adapted Games and Puzzles Games that encourage turn-taking, teamwork, and fun are wonderful. Modify rules or choose puzzles that align with your child’s interests and abilities. Read More: Indoor Destinations in New York: Safe Spaces for Children with Autism Holiday Tips for Parents of Children with Autism In addition to activities, here are some supportive strategies for making the holidays smoother: Prepare with visuals: Use calendars or picture schedules to show what’s coming. Keep routines in place: Maintain mealtimes and bedtime as much as possible. Offer choices: Allow children to pick activities, decorations, or songs. Plan for breaks: Schedule downtime during busy days to help prevent overwhelm. Stay flexible: Not every activity will go as planned, and that’s okay. These holiday tips for children with autism help families balance fun with predictability.
